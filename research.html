Research Statements

Key Motivation: "What makes a good Optimizer?"
Training neural network (NN) models is an essential component of the deep learning pipeline, and optimization algorithms play a pivotal role in this process.

The Current Challenge

To date, hundreds of optimizers have been proposed to the deep learning communityâ€”with more being added every year. We have now entered an era where, amidst this overwhelming flood of options, it has become exceedingly difficult to determine if one algorithm is better than another, let alone which one is state-of-the-art (SOTA).

I believe this issue ultimately stems from the fact that the deep learning community lacks a clear, theoretically and practically useful definition of what constitutes a "good optimizer."

Fundamental Questions

Convergence vs Performance: Does a faster convergence rate (or better iteration complexity, or a tighter regret bound in online learning) necessarily mean it's a better algorithm?

Efficiency Metrics: Is an algorithm truly superior if it achieves the same training loss in fewer iterations, regardless of its number of hyperparameters, computational cost (FLOPs), or wall-clock time?

Research Direction

The optimization community has recently been producing a wide range of research to fundamentally explore these bottlenecks. My goal is to contribute a new milestone to this ongoing effort.

Core Research Objectives

1
Define Excellence: Clearly defining what makes an optimizer "good," both mathematically and practically.
2
Evaluate Existing Methods: Identifying which existing algorithms are superior under this new definition.
3
Innovate Solutions: Developing new and improved optimizers based on these findings.
